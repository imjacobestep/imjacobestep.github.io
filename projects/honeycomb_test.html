Honeycomb

Honeycomb is an iOS app designed as a hub for Mary's Place Seattle to organize, explore, and share community resources. This was created as my graduate launch project at GIX, sponsored by Starbucks and Mary's Place. The objective was to help MP's Outreach and Diversion team more easily connect families in need with community resources.

Background

Who is Mary's Place?

Mary's Place is a Seattle-based non-profit dedicated to providing safety, stability, and hope to families experiencing homelessness.

How did I get this project?

At GIX, sponsors pitch their projects in a weekly studio class during our Spring quarter. Mary's Place pitched at my first class meeting, and I immediately knew it was the launch project for me. I grew up lower income in rural West Virginia; this topic was near to my heart. More than anything, I wanted to apply my skills to something that would actually impact real people, not just build a flashy tech demo.

Who did I work with?

Team Honeycomb consisted of Abhijeet Saraf (Lead UX Designer), Saif Mustafa (PM), Shih-Hsin Lo (Back-end Engineer), and myself (Front-End Engineer). We each had informal titles but, with only 4 of us and a first quarter entirely dedicated to research, we all wore many hats.

Secondary Research

We weren't given a specific problem to solve, so we began our project by combing through Mary's Place's current intake statistics, org chart, and new-hire training material. We also met with MP's Data Team to learn what tools they use, what data is collected, and how we might leverage that to lower family wait times. At this point, we assumed we would be building a new intake form that algorithmically produced a list of known resources a family would likely be eligible for.

Primary Research

Exploring Outreach

We developed a rough user flow diagram tracing family experiences, but org charts and brief meetings only gave us so much insight into MP's internal workings. To learn more, we observed an outreach where specialists assisted a family of ten living in an SUV, interviewed the outreach manager, and conducted a focus group with five outreach specialists. To close both interviews, we had participants annotate our user flow diagram, correcting any mistakes and highlighting pain points.

Questions

  • Is our user-flow correct and complete?

  • What common issues arise with the current process?

  • How to the outreach and intake teams coordinate?

  • What resources are commonly asked for?

  • How are families prioritized or determined eligible?

Findings

Specialists are each assigned 15 families per week

Specialists spend most time trying to find accurate information on resources

MP helps with so much more than just shelter

Fragmented shared knowledge of resources

Most resource-sharing is verbal

Families contact intake, who refers those eligible to outreach

Resources are complex, may have multiple categories

Exploring Intake

In our focus group, specialists mentioned that the intake team also uses outreach's main online tool for onboarding families. To make sure we understood the intake process and gauge whether our solution would impact the intake team, we interviewed the intake manager and had her give us a technical walkthrough of the intake process.

Analysis

To process what we'd learned and identify patterns, we used an affinity analysis technique: sticky notes everywhere! We grouped our notes, labelled high-impact pain points, and discovered our problem.

How can we help outreach specialists explore, document, utilize, and collaborate on community resource information?
